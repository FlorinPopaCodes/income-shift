## Design Context

### Users
People encountering this chart on social media or in a presentation deck. They may not be economists — the chart needs to communicate clearly in seconds, with enough depth to reward closer inspection. Will likely be viewed on screens of varying sizes, possibly as a screenshot.

### Brand Personality
Credible, clear, understated.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Evolve from FT style**: Keep the warmth and typographic quality of the FT-inspired palette (salmon, serif headings, sans body) but push toward something more original — less "FT clone," more "informed by the same tradition."
- **Tone**: Neutral and academic. Let the data make the argument. Avoid editorial flourishes or dramatic visual framing.
- **Anti-references**: Overly decorative infographics, dark dashboards, generic chart.js defaults.
- **Theme**: Light mode only. Warm neutrals, muted accent color, generous whitespace.

### Design Principles
1. **Data integrity first** — Never distort perception. Use density for variable-width bins. Label axes clearly. Show methodology.
2. **Clarity over decoration** — Every visual element should reduce ambiguity, not add flair. If it doesn't help the reader understand the data, remove it.
3. **Readable at a glance, rich on inspection** — The main story (distribution shift over 50 years) should land in 3 seconds. Stats, controls, and methodology reward closer reading.
4. **Typographic hierarchy** — Use weight, size, and font family (serif for emphasis, sans for data) to guide the eye. Avoid color as the primary hierarchy tool.
5. **Warmth without personality** — The palette should feel approachable and high-quality (not cold/corporate, not playful/branded). Academic confidence.
